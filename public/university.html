1. Welcome to Year 2 ▼

Welcome — Dr. A. Okonkwo, Programme Leader

Welcome to Year 2 of the BEng (Hons) Biomedical Engineering programme. Having established the foundational principles of engineering science, anatomy, physiology, and materials behaviour in Year 1, you will now move into applied and specialist territory. Year 2 is the point at which the programme begins to look and feel like professional biomedical engineering practice: you will be working with real physiological data, real biological samples, and real engineering constraints.

The modules this year have been designed to build on each other progressively. Your work in Biomaterials and Tissue Interaction will inform your approach to the Medical Device Design project; your laboratory work in Biological Systems will provide context for both the Sensors module and your Independent Study. Please engage with all modules concurrently rather than sequentially — the connections between them are deliberate and important.

Year 2 carries 120 credits and contributes 30% toward your final degree classification. The year culminates in a Group Design Project (BME2005), which is assessed both on your technical output and on your individual contribution to the team process. Previous cohorts have found this module simultaneously the most demanding and most rewarding of the year. Start early.

📌 Placement Year (Year 3): Applications for the optional industrial placement year open in March. Placements are competitive. Students wishing to be considered should ensure their CV and covering letter materials are ready by end of Semester 2. Speak to the Careers and Placement team in the John Dalton Building. A number of placements with life sciences and biomedical device companies have been circulated via the departmental notice board.

3. Module in Focus: BME2003 — Biological Systems Engineering ▼

BME2003: Biological Systems Engineering

This module introduces you to the analysis and engineering of biological systems at scales ranging from the subcellular to the whole organism. It is deliberately broader in scope than the other Year 2 modules, reflecting the fact that modern biomedical engineering increasingly requires engineers to understand, model, and in some cases design biological processes — not merely to interface with them.

The module is structured in four thematic blocks:

Block A — Biological Systems as Engineering Systems (Weeks 1–3)

An introduction to systems thinking applied to biology. Feedback loops, homeostasis, signal transduction. The cell as a system: membrane dynamics, metabolic regulation, mechanosensing. Students will be expected to construct simple mathematical models of regulatory systems using MATLAB.

Block B — Tissue and Organism-Level Mechanics (Weeks 4–6)

Mechanical behaviour of soft biological tissue. Viscoelasticity, anisotropy, and stress response in living systems. Wound healing as an engineering problem. Introduction to mechanobiology and the role of physical forces in gene expression and tissue organisation.

Block C — Synthetic Biology and Engineered Systems (Weeks 7–9)

The principles and practice of designing biological systems for specific outputs. Genetic circuits, standardised biological parts, chassis organisms. Case studies drawn from pharmaceutical manufacturing, biosensor development, and industrial biotechnology. Students will analyse and critique a real-world synthetic biology application in a structured written exercise.

This block includes a guest lecture from a practicing industrial biotechnologist. Details to follow.

Block D — Systems Failure, Adaptation, and Emergent Behaviour (Weeks 10–12)

What happens when biological systems do not behave as designed or predicted. Evolutionary pressure and genetic drift in engineered organisms. Emergent behaviours at population scale. Ethical and regulatory considerations in the deployment of engineered biological systems in open environments. Students will engage with a case study of a biological system whose industrial application produced unanticipated ecological outcomes.

⚠ Note on Block D: The case study material for Block D is drawn from real-world examples and will include scenarios involving unintended environmental release and population-scale ecological impact. Students who have concerns about the content of this material should contact Dr. Okonkwo in advance of Week 10.
